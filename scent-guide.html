The Scent Journal

Notes on memory, fragrance, and how to pick a scent that belongs to you.

How to Choose a Fragrance

Forget everything you have read about notes and sillage and projection. That is the language of industry, not feeling. The only question worth asking when you smell something is: What does this remind me of?

A scent does not need to be pretty. It needs to be true. It needs to pull something up from your chest. A place, a person, a version of yourself you had forgotten. That is how you know it is yours.

Fragrance Families

Woody and Smoked

Cedar, sandalwood, vetiver, tobacco, leather. These are the scents of old libraries, winter coats, and hands that have worked. They are grounding. Quiet. Patient. Best worn when you need to feel anchored.

Floral and Green

Rose, iris, violet leaf, nettle, wet earth. Not the sweet florals you find at mass-market perfume counters. Green, living florals. The smell of a garden after rain. These scents are intimate, nostalgic, deeply personal.

Fresh and Airy

Lime, sea salt, linen, white musk. The scents of morning. Light through a window. A letter written and left in the sun. Clean without being sterile. Fresh without being loud.

Dark and Resinous

Incense, myrrh, frankincense, clove, charcoal. Ancient. Sacred. These are the scents of ritual and reverence. They demand presence. Wear them when you want to feel the weight of a moment.

How to Wear Perfume

I do not believe in rules about pulse points and wrists. Apply fragrance where you will find it during your day. The inside of your collar. The hem of your sleeve. The corner of a scarf. A perfume should be discovered, not announced.

If you put on a fragrance and forget it is there, then catch it again hours later and smile. That is a successful wearing.

How to Use a Reed Diffuser

Place your diffuser where air moves. Near a window, a doorway, a hallway. The reeds draw the oil up and release it slowly, like a breath held and released. Flip the reeds once a week to refresh the intensity. A good diffuser lasts 8 to 12 weeks, changing subtly as it ages. The final weeks are often the most beautiful. Softer. Quieter. Like the end of a story.

A Note on Memory

Every fragrance I make starts with a memory. Not a brief from a marketing team. Not a trend report. A moment. A specific, irreplaceable moment that marked me. When you wear Miss Vee, you are not wearing a product. You are wearing a moment that someone loved enough to try and capture.

I hope it becomes part of your memories too.
